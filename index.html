Chosen biome

Tropical rainforest: a living food engine.

Tropical rainforests are warm, wet, layered ecosystems near the equator. Their food value comes from more than harvests: they store genetic diversity, shelter pollinators, recycle rainfall, protect soils, and support communities who depend on forest products and forest-friendly farming.

FAO reports that forests cover about 31 percent of Earth's land area and that people benefit from forest biodiversity through carbon, water, nutrient cycles, and links with food production.

Food production

The canopy feeds cities you have never seen.

Many rainforest foods are global staples or high-value crops. Cocoa becomes chocolate. Coffee cherries become morning routines. Bananas travel through global markets. Cassava feeds millions in the tropics. Forest fruits, nuts, oils, spices, and medicines add more layers to the biome's value.

Current threats

The same demand for food can damage the biome that feeds us.

FAO estimates that 420 million hectares of forest have been lost since 1990 through conversion to other land uses. From 2015 to 2020, deforestation was about 10 million hectares per year.

Agricultural expansion is the main driver. Between 2000 and 2010, large-scale commercial agriculture accounted for about 40 percent of tropical deforestation, while local subsistence agriculture accounted for another 33 percent.

01

Forest conversion

Forest is cleared for cattle, soy, oil palm, roads, mining, and expanding settlements. This removes habitat and breaks food webs.

02

Monoculture farming

Single-crop plantations can replace diverse forest layers, reducing pollinators, soil organisms, and long-term crop resilience.

03

Climate stress

Hotter temperatures, drought, extreme rain, and fire make crops such as cocoa and coffee more vulnerable to disease and failed harvests.

04

Soil and water loss

When canopy cover disappears, rain hits bare soil directly. Erosion strips nutrients and muddies rivers that farms and communities need.

Sustainable management

A food future that keeps the forest standing.

The goal is not to lock people out of rainforests. It is to manage landscapes so food, income, biodiversity, and climate stability can survive together.

Grow in layers

Use agroforestry: cocoa, coffee, fruits, and spices can grow under shade trees, imitating forest structure and protecting soil.

Protect intact forest

Strong protected areas, land-tenure security, and respect for Indigenous and local knowledge reduce destructive clearing.

Restore degraded land

Replant native trees, reconnect corridors, and improve exhausted farmland instead of clearing new forest.

Buy deforestation-free

Companies and consumers can demand cocoa, coffee, palm oil, and other products that do not come from newly cleared forest.

Waste less food

Less waste lowers pressure to convert more forest into farms, which protects the biome and the food system.

Final message

Feed people without eating the forest.

Tropical rainforests are food producers, water machines, climate regulators, genetic libraries, and homes. The future of rainforest food is not bigger scars. It is smarter landscapes.
